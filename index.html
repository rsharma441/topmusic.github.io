Top Albums of 2021

And here they are, starting with #25. You can click the Album image to get a Spotify/YouTube Link. Also, the email gets cut off so you'll have to click "View entired message"
25. Cherry - Anna Fox Rochinski

(indie rock) Most definitely a whimsical transformation from Rochinski’s work with Quilt. The masterful use of random, unexpected instrumentation gives it almost a child-like rollick. Great if you like: The Shins or Spoon
 
24. Mythopoetics - Half Waif

(indie rock/electronic) A successfully ambitious expedition leaving listeners absorbed by their own haunting questions. Masterful usage of electrostatics, polyphony make such a deep soundscape.  Great if you like: Radiohead or Laura Marling
 
23. Yellow - Emma-Jean Thackray

(jazz) A determined jazz album taking influence from beat splicing DJs like Flying Lotus and more sonorous Alice Coltrane. It hangs on by a thread making it all the more exciting. Great if you like: Alice Coltrane, Pharoah Sanders, Sun Ra, Charles Mingus
22. Doomin Sun - Bachelor

(indie rock) A curative interchange between Melina Duterte, of Jay Som, and Palehound’s Ellen Kempner, over relationships and frustration. Preeminent songwriters working carefully to meld their craft together. Great if you like: Spoon, Superchunk, Sebadoh

21. Planet Her - Doja Cat

(pop/r&b) Beyond simply being one of the most popular musicians, Doja Cat is pushing the boundaries of pop, both thematically through colorful celebrations of BIPOC women and textured music reflecting internet 8-bit culture. Great if you like: Ariana Grande, Beyonce, Fergie  
20. Vulture Prince - Arooj Aftab

(electronic) - Delicate, yet not fragile. Capturing the nostalgia of Pakistani or Indian styled ghazals but freely wandering the New York streets and picking up influences from all corners of the world. Great if you like: Dido, Brian Eno, The Chemical Brothers
19. Cool - Colleen Green

(indie rock)  Though she describes the “cool”-ness as a gimmick, this album substantiates its claim. It is candid and charming and underneath its maturity is a skeleton of her earlier punk work. Great if you like: that dog, La Sera, Pavement, Liz Phair
18. Smiling With No Teeth - Genesis Owusu

(r&b/hip hop) A jubilant and charismatic work that pulls in all sorts of direction. It carefully mixes the analog and digital worlds and never misses a chance to add flavor to some already well written songs. Great if you like: Prince, GoldLink, J Dilla 
17. Juno - Remi Wolf

(indie pop/hip hop)  Remi Wolf’s zany take on pop and hip hop makes for one of the most enthusiastic, gregarious and, of course, danceable, bops of the year. It’s free range jazz meeting pop. Great if you like: Charli XCX, M.I.A., Janelle Monae
16. KIDS - Noga Erez

(electronic/indie pop) The second release from this imaginative Israeli electronic artist refuses to take a break. It’s witty and rhythmic throughout. Definitely a strut. Great if you like: Billie Eillish, Lorde, Ashe

15.When Smoke Rises - Mustafa

(r&b) Within seconds of listening, the heartfelt grief in Mustafa’s voice vibrates throughout your body. Floating on top of sonorous emotional instrumentation, makes for one of the most evocative pieces I’ve ever heard. Great if you like: Vance Joy, D’Angelo, Sampha, James Blake

14. Head of Roses - Flock of Dimes

(indie rock/electronic) Jenn Wasner does such an excellent job building the guitar and her synth around her voice, it almost feels a part of it. Great if you like: Lorde, HAIM, St. Vincent, Norah Jones
13. The Keeper - Maja Lena

 (indie folk) What a beautiful pure sound that Maja Lena puts forth. She clearly draws her power from Appalachian folk but mainlines it with clear nods to her Scandinavian heritage with melody lines that are really only heard in Nordic folk songs. That balance makes it feel almost cosmic or otherworldly. Great if you like: Joanna Newsom, Joni Mitchell, Fionna Apple, Kate Bush
 
12. Town’s Dead - Kojaque

(hip hop) Truth be told, I am not the biggest grime fan, but this album transcends that or any genre, by breaking out of typical grime themes and rhythms. In doing so, it manifests an experimental form, with clever beats, effective interludes and outros, and a thoughtful pushing and pulling of its topical agenda.  Great if you like: Kendrick Lamar, Beastie Boys, Jurassic 5
11. Really From - Really From

(jazz/indie folk) It’s not often you hear a band that can take sophisticated jazz runs and laminate it with a distressing punk ethos, callback lo-fi indie math-rock, and then resolve to anthemic pop, but that’s exactly what this Boston group does with ease. Great if you like: American Football, Don Caballero, Mitski

10. Animal - Lump

(indie folk/indie pop)  A curvaceous and fearless demonstration of pop that manages to elude any clear expectations. Laura Marling and Mike Lindsay show masterful chops with how confidently they approach dissonance, lo-fi production and even odd rhythmic choices yet still keep the music very easy to feel. Great if you like: Radiohead, Arcade Fire, PJ Harvey, Slowdive
9. Promises - Floating Points, London Symphony Orchestra, and Pharoah Sanders

(jazz) Few albums have captured abstract storytelling as well as this nine movement piece by Pharaoah Sanders and Floating Points. It’s like a sequence of dreams that continues to unfold with every listen, emphasizing some seriously unreplicable improvisation. Great if you like: Miles Davis, Ornette Coleman, John Coltrane
8. Don’t Go Tellin’ Your Momma - Topaz Jones

(hip hop/r&b)  The son of an activist and jazz musician, Jones, is retelling stories of his life. What makes it so relatable is aided by the perfect balance of relaxed funk-adorned beats and his quick, vibrant and convivial rhymes. Great if you like: P. Diddy, Kanye West, Childish Gambino
7. The Shadow I Remember - Cloud Nothings

(punk) Reappearing on my list after almost 10 years, the Cloud Nothings still get their hands dirty like they do on their now modern post-punk revival classic, Attack on Memory, but they also reflect on the malaise of pandemic life with mature perspective. Great if you like: Fugazi, Bad Brains, Japandroids
6. Cheater - Pom Poko

(indie rock/punk) The sophomoric release for these Norwegian punkers successfully attempt unwonted gambits among familiar danceable resolutions. Expect the unexpected. Great if you like:  Laura Stevenson and the Cans, Jeff Rosenstock, Sleater-Kinney, PJ Harvey
5. Deja - Bomba Estéreo

(electronic/cumbia) Clearly themed around Colombian protest this album embraces natural sounds, acoustic instruments, almost as if to deject the perils of modern human society. Though it is enticingly danceable, it also maintains sagacious wisdom. Great if you like: Jamie XX, Ana Tijoux, Lido Pimienta
4. Nine - Sault

(hip hop) - One of the most mysterious modern music outfits, Sault put out this album for simply 99 days and then removed it from streaming. Mysterious, borderline Cronenbergian horror, but definitely some of the most interesting takes on hip hop I’ve ever heard. Great if you like: OutKast, the clipping. , Tyler, the Creator
3. Wayfinder - Boy Scouts

(indie rock/indie folk) An album that pulls at the heartstrings but almost always offers a reward. Taylor Vick’s mystically ominous melodies, almost grunge-like in that they sustain off-pitch, are often met with satisfying fat guitar chords keep everything in check. Great if you like: Phoebe Bridgers, Big Thief, Iron and Wine

1. Call Me If You Get Lost - Tyler, The Creator

(hip hop) To me, a masterpiece hip-hop album should have two things: credibility and creativity. Hip hop has always been something to gather people, giving good hip-hop credibility. As time has gone on, artists have expanded the genre through creative choices. The Beastie Boys did it with Paul’s Boutique and Jay-Z with The Blueprint. Tyler, and co, have always had the ability to do either of those things really well, and often both, but this time it rose to a new height as an entire album from top-to-bottom. Tyler has often done production himself, but the sheer variety of soundscapes– from 90s r&b nostalgia, to 2000s mafioso swagger, to quintessential only Odd-Future could do with lo-fi indie cypher sounds, is awe-inspiring. Beyond how good it feels to hear those beats and hear his unique flow rapping over them, Tyler has become such an important voice at various points of his stardom and I believe he cashes in on all of that to cement himself as a timelines legend in rap with his messaging and self-awareness and self-doubt on this album. Thanks, Tyler, for making it look so cool to be so unsure and just try. Great if you like: A$AP Rocky, Future, 50 Cent, Dr. Dre
1. Laura Stevenson - Laura Stevenson

(indie rock) Now, for pretty much as long as I’ve done this list, Laura Stevenson has always been on it. She’s simply one of the most clever songwriters that’s walked this earth. The pinpoint accuracy with which she can re-imagine an human emotion as a melody, then set the scene with the most poignant attention to detail– whether it be how she enunciates a phrase, what arpeggio by which a guitar chord progression is awoken, a rest, every detail is meaningful. This album took me time to really become comfortable with, in part, due to how beautifully Stevenson masks the meaning through metaphor. That difficulty only helped to sweeten the reward, giving legs to the cliche “not the destination but the journey." Like for many of you, this year was difficult, not just for the things happening outside of our homes, but for whom we all spent most of it with– ourselves. So, thank you Laura Stevenson, I really like who you’re helping me learn who I am coming to be. Great if you like: Brandi Carlile, Waxahatchee, The Decembrists, Bright Eyes
